## The Size of Atomic Space

Atoms are the building blocks of matter, and they come in different shapes and sizes. The human body is made up of approximately 37.2 trillion cells, each containing trillions of atoms. But the size of these atoms is what's truly astonishing. On average, the diameter of a human atom is about 1-2 angstroms (Å). To put that into perspective, 1 Å is equivalent to 0.1 nanometers (nm). Now, imagine packing thousands of these tiny atoms into a single cell.

## The Weird World of Atomic Packing

You see, the atoms in our bodies are arranged in a specific pattern, known as a crystal lattice structure. This structure is what gives our bones their strength and rigidity. But what if we were to remove all the empty space from these crystals? We'd essentially be packing our atoms into a dense, compact sphere. And that's where the math gets interesting.

## The Math of Atom Packing

According to a 2012 study published in the Journal of Applied Physics, the packing efficiency of atomic structures is around 64% for face-centered cubic (FCC) lattices, which are the most common type of lattice found in many materials. ## The Weird Science of Human Density

So, how dense would we be if we were packed like a sugar cube? Well, the density of a sugar cube is around 1.5-2.5 g/cm³, depending on its moisture content. If we were to remove all the empty space from our atoms and fit ourselves inside a cube with a volume of 4.43 grams, our density would be roughly equivalent to that of lead. That's right, folks, we'd be almost as dense as the toxic metal itself.
