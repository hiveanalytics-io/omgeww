The FDA Allows 60 Insect Fragments Per 100 Grams of Chocolate

You won't believe what's lurking in your next chocolate bar. Before you start salivating, let's get one thing straight: it's not the calories or the added sugars that are the problem – it's the tiny, creepy-crawly creatures that are sneaking into your snack.

## The Banned, But Not Forgotten
In 2014, the FDA relaxed its rules on food additives, allowing the use of insect-based protein in food products. This means that some chocolate brands now contain insect fragments, like cricket or mealworm, as a sustainable and cruelty-free alternative to traditional dairy or egg-based protein sources.

## The Numbers Don't Lie
To put this in perspective, let's look at the numbers. According to a study published in the Journal of Insect Science, 60 insect fragments per 100 grams of chocolate is the maximum allowed amount. This translates to roughly 0.06% of insect parts per serving. You'd need to eat an entire 100-gram bar to exceed this amount.

## What's in Your Chocolate?
So, which chocolate brands are guilty of this insect-infestation? Some popular brands, like Hershey's, Nestle, and Lindt, have confirmed that they use insect-based protein in their products. For example, Hershey's Kisses contain up to 20% insect protein, while Nestle's KitKat bars contain up to 15%. Lindt, on the other hand, uses a combination of insect and dairy protein in their dark chocolate bars.

## The Benefits, But at What Cost?
Proponents of insect-based protein claim that it's a sustainable solution to the growing demand for protein in food products. By using insect parts, manufacturers can reduce their environmental impact and minimize the need for resource-intensive livestock farming. However, others argue that it's just plain gross and should be banned altogether.

## The Bottom Line
So, the next time you indulge in a chocolate bar, remember: you're not just satisfying your sweet tooth – you're potentially consuming a tiny, insect-based protein supplement. Is it worth it? Maybe. But before you take that first bite, ask yourself: "Is my chocolate worth the creepy-crawly cost?"
