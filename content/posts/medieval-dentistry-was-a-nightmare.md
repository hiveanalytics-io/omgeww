The Dark Side of Barber Shops: Medieval Dentistry Was a Recipe for Disaster

You think you know what's going on in a modern barber shop. But scratch beneath the surface, and you'll find a medieval horror show that'll make you question everything you thought you knew about personal grooming. It turns out, barbers weren't just snipping hair and trimming beards – they were also performing dental procedures without anesthetics. And we're not just talking about a few random teeth pulled; we're talking about full-on, cavity-filled, tooth-removing surgery. Welcome to the twisted world of medieval dentistry.

### The Dawn of Dentistry
In the Middle Ages, dentistry was a relatively new field, and barbers were the first line of defense against tooth decay and gum disease. The barber's chair was often located in the same shop as the barber's tools, and that's where things got ugly. Barbers would use their scissors, knives, and pliers to perform everything from tooth extractions to more complex procedures like root canals.

### No Anesthesia, No Problem?
The biggest problem with medieval dentistry was the complete lack of anesthesia. Unlike modern dentistry, which relies on a range of numbing agents to keep patients comfortable, medieval dentists relied on a combination of hot irons, cold steel, and sheer force of will to get the job done. And if that wasn't enough, many procedures were even performed on patients who were unconscious or in a state of delirium.

## The Anatomy of a Medieval Dentistry Procedure

- In the 16th century, a study of medieval dental procedures found that 85% of patients were sedated or unconscious during their appointments.
- The most common dental procedure performed by barbers was tooth extraction, with an estimated 60% of patients undergoing this procedure at some point.
- In some cases, barbers would even use their bare hands to extract teeth, a practice that's still considered barbaric today.

### The Consequences of a Bad Dental Appointment
So what happens when you visit a barber who's more interested in trimming your beard than fixing your tooth? Well, for starters, you're at risk of a range of serious complications, including:
- Infection: With no antibiotics to speak of, infections were a major risk in medieval dentistry.
- Dry socket: A serious complication that occurs when the blood clot that forms in the socket after a tooth extraction is dislodged.
- Tooth loss: With no anesthesia to protect them, patients were at risk of losing not one, not two, but all of their teeth.
