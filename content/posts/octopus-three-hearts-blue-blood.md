You won't believe what's pumping through the veins of the ocean's most mysterious creatures. Octopuses, those eight-armed masterminds, have three hearts beating in their bodies – and it's not just a myth. In fact, they have a total of three hearts, with the third one being a branchial heart that pumps blood to their gills.

## The Bloody Truth

Octopuses have blue blood, just like some humans have red blood. The key difference is that octopus blood is copper-based, which is much more efficient for transporting oxygen to their cells. This is because copper is a more potent oxygen carrier than iron, which is the basis for human red blood cells. The copper-based blood also gives octopuses their characteristic blue color – a trait that's both fascinating and a little unsettling.

## A Brain for Every Body Part

Octopuses have a whopping nine brains, but they're not just clustered in their heads like ours. Each arm has its own mini-brain, known as a ganglion, which controls movement and sensation. These ganglia are highly specialized and can function independently of the main brain, allowing the octopus to solve problems and react to stimuli without needing to send signals all the way back to the central nervous system. It's like they have nine tiny, squirming overlords controlling their every move.

## A Long and Eventful Life

Octopuses have a relatively short lifespan, typically ranging from one to five years in the wild. However, some species have been known to live up to 10 years in captivity, thanks to advances in aquarium care and nutrition. Despite their short lives, octopuses have a remarkable ability to adapt and change color, with some species able to mimic their surroundings in a split second.

## The Most Intelligent Invertebrate on the Planet

Octopuses have been observed using tools, solving complex puzzles, and even displaying a form of playfulness. In 2018, a group of octopuses was observed opening jars to get to food – a feat that's usually the domain of primates and dolphins. These intelligent creatures have been a subject of fascination for scientists and aquarium enthusiasts alike, with many considering them the most intelligent invertebrate on the planet.
