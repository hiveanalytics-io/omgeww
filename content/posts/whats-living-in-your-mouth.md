Meet the microscopic roommates that live in your mouth, and they're not as friendly as you think. In fact, they're so numerous that they outnumber human cells 10 to 1 – and they're still not fully understood.

## The Uninvited Guests
It's estimated that 700 different species of bacteria call the human mouth home, with some sources suggesting as many as 1,000 different types. These tiny tenants come from a variety of places, including our food, the air we breathe, and even our own bodies. And once they're inside, they're in for the long haul – with some species thought to live in the mouth for decades.

## The Oral Hygiene Myth
We've all been told that brushing our teeth regularly is enough to keep the bacteria at bay. But the truth is, no amount of toothpaste or flossing can completely eliminate the risk of mouth-borne illness. In fact, studies have shown that up to 50% of the bacteria in our mouths are resistant to antibiotics. So, what's the point of brushing if the bacteria are just going to come back?

## The Sugar Connection
One of the main ways that bacteria thrive in our mouths is by consuming sugar. When we eat sugary snacks or drinks, the bacteria feast on the sugars and multiply at an alarming rate. This can lead to a host of problems, including tooth decay, gum disease, and even heart disease. The problem is, we're not even aware of it – most of us don't floss or brush our teeth often enough to keep the bacteria in check.

## The Forgotten Microbes
Despite their importance, the bacteria living in our mouths are still not fully understood. In fact, it's estimated that up to 75% of the bacteria in our mouths are still unknown to science. This is partly due to the fact that they're incredibly tiny and difficult to study. But researchers are starting to make some groundbreaking discoveries about these microscopic roommates – and they're shedding new light on the importance of oral hygiene.

## The Bottom Line
So, the next time you brush your teeth or floss, remember that you're not just getting rid of yesterday's food particles – you're also fighting off a tiny army of roommates who are trying to take over your mouth. And the next time you eat a sugary snack, don't be surprised if the bacteria in your mouth start to get a little too comfortable. Because the truth is, your mouth is a microbial wonderland – and you're just a guest in someone else's home.
