Yellowstone's Supervolcano Is "Overdue" — What Would Actually Happen

You've probably heard the rumors: Yellowstone's supervolcano is due for a massive eruption. The question is, what would it actually look like? Would it be the end of the world, or just a really bad day?

The short answer is, we're not entirely sure. But scientists have been warning us about the dangers of a Yellowstone eruption for decades. The most shocking fact? Yellowstone sits atop a 30-mile-wide caldera, which was formed when a massive volcano erupted about 640,000 years ago.

## What Would Happen to the Planet?

A Yellowstone eruption would release an estimated 2,500 megatons of TNT into the atmosphere, which is roughly 100 times more powerful than the Hiroshima bomb. To put that in perspective, the explosion would be so massive that it would:

* Change the Earth's climate, causing a prolonged "volcanic winter" that could last for years
* Release massive amounts of ash and gas into the atmosphere, blocking out sunlight and affecting global temperatures
* Trigger massive earthquakes and tremors, potentially even causing a 10-foot tsunami in the nearby Yellowstone River

## When Would It Happen?

So when exactly would a Yellowstone eruption happen? Well, the answer is, we don't know. Scientists have been monitoring the volcano's activity for decades, but there are still many unknowns. In fact, the United States Geological Survey (USGS) estimates that the probability of a major eruption within the next 10,000 years is relatively low – about 1 in 730,000.

## What Would the Aftermath Look Like?

So what would the world look like after a Yellowstone eruption? It would be a desolate, barren wasteland. The ash and gas released into the atmosphere would cause widespread crop failures, leading to food shortages and economic collapse. The planet's climate would be severely disrupted, with temperatures potentially dropping by as much as 20 degrees Celsius.

## The Final Showdown

But here's the thing: a Yellowstone eruption is not a matter of "if," but "when." The volcano is still active, and scientists estimate that it's overdue for a major eruption. In fact, the USGS says that Yellowstone is due for its next eruption in about 10 million years. But would we be around to see it? Probably not.
