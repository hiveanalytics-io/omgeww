In the first stage of a butterfly's life, the caterpillar is the only stage that's visible. But what happens when it's time for a transformation? The caterpillar undergoes a radical change, dissolving into a liquid called a "larval fluid" before forming a chrysalis.

### The Science Behind the Soup

When a caterpillar is ready to transform, it releases enzymes that break down its body into a liquid, called "larval fluid." This fluid is made up of nutrients and waste products that the caterpillar has stored up over its lifetime. The liquid is then pumped into the chrysalis, where it's broken down further by enzymes, turning it into a soup-like substance.

### Chrysalis: The Cocoon of Transformation

The chrysalis is a protective casing that holds the caterpillar's body while it undergoes metamorphosis. Inside the chrysalis, the caterpillar's body breaks down into a soup-like substance that's 75% water and 25% enzymes. This substance is then reorganized into the adult butterfly's body parts, including its wings, legs, and organs.
